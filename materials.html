Taking a Walk

Taking a walk can be a great way to give both of you a chance to talk about how you've been feeling lately and reflect on your relationship together.

  • Time for your longer conversation with your friend? Need some guidelines to help you begin sharing your reflections? We suggest starting out with a nice walk with your friend and having a conversation as you walk. Going on a stroll while you talk can provide a lot of benefits. It can create an environment that fosters comfort and safety, creative thinking, and an adjustment in perspective, which will all great for talking through any difficult subjects.

  • Be sure to pick a walking route through environments that both you and your friend are comfortable with. Here are some general types of environments and how they could be helpful:

    • Natural Setting: We are so often in a man-made environment, so changing that to a natural environment can revitalize and rejuvenate us. There are also more open spaces so you and your friend can feel less confined and more free to express yourselves. And for subjects that can become heated or emotionally charged, the environment can serve to calm your nerves.
    • Urban Setting: Even though this setting can cause more stimulation, you or friend may feel comfortable in the feeling of having a private conversation but still not feeling too alone in the vicinity of nearby people.
    • Indoors Setting: Although ideally the walk should take place outdoor, and indoor location could also be fine if it is wide and spacious. Take for instance, a wide hallway or a convention center.

    • Walking can take you and your friend away from the routines and habits you normally take on in your everyday life.
    • Several studies have been done linking creative thinking to walking. While this may be beneficial all on its own, it will surely help you and your friend think of creative ways to overcome any issues you have.

    • You can be spontaneou on where you want to go as a destination or pick your location. Roaming around on a walking route may help you achieve the sense of being free and having a fixed location may give you and your friend something to look forward to at the end of your walk.
    • The pacing of your walk also presents an interesting aspect. For instance, you can choose to quicken or slow your pace to the timing of your thoughts. This could actually make an impact on your mood and how fast your thoughts flow. At a serious moment with your friend, you can even stop your pace to communicate your thoughts.

  • All in all, be sure to create a comfortable environment and give yourselves some flexibility as well. This should be an enjoyable conversation and a chance to reflect and share some of your thoughts with each other.

Topics

These are some ideas to keep in mind while having your conversation.

  • Conversations flow when you are curious about the person you are talking to. No one likes to speak to someone who is not listening. So, be sure to smile, make eye contact, ask follow-up questions, and be present.

    • What has changed in your life since the last time we spoke?
    • What have you wanted to share with someone, but no one has asked you about it yet?
    • What have you been doing for fun lately?
    • What plans do you have in the works?

    "You can make more friends in two months by becoming interested in other people than you can in two years by trying to get other people interested in you."

  • Appreciation whether through a compliment or another form of recognition can go a long way. It takes little effort to notice something someone has put a lot of effort into.

    • I really liked how today...
    • I thought of you when...

    "Once I did bad and that I heard ever. Twice I did good, but that I heard never."

  • Don’t assume you know what is on another person’s mind. Ask them and know for sure. Only when you understand can you move forward.

    • Help me understand why you feel the way you do
    • What do you want from this coversation?

    “Personally I am very fond of strawberries and cream, but I have found that for some strange reason, fish prefer worms. So when I went fishing, I didn’t think about what I wanted. I thought about what they wanted. I didn't bait the hook with strawberries and cream. Rather, I dangled a worm or grasshopper in front of the fish and said: "Wouldn't you like to have that?"Why not use the same common sense when fishing for people?”

  • You can't predict exactly how somebody else is going to react to anything, much as you might try. This can make many things scary, especially when you imagine them reacting horribly to something you do or say. Just remember that all you can control is yourself. You can consider what you might do in a couple of scenarios, but ultimately, their actions are their own.

    • Consider the best scenario. What would you do?
    • Consider the worst scenario. What would you do?
    • Feel Free!
    • Keep this fear in mind when reacting to things that other people say as well.

    "Learning that you can’t control the other person’s reaction, and that it can be destructive to try, can be incredibly liberating. It not only gives the other person the space to react however they need to, but also takes a huge amount of pressure off you. You will learn things about yourself based on their reaction, but if you are prepared to learn, you’ll feel free from the desperate need for their reaction to go one certain way."

  • It always feels horrible when a friend is angry at you. The first step towards receiving forgiveness for anything you've done is giving that forgiveness yourself. You and your friend will both feel better for it.

    • You can be the bigger person!

    "Any fool can criticize, complain, and condemn—and most fools do. But it takes character and self-control to be understanding and forgiving."

Activities

If you're looking for some ideas for activities to help you and your friend express what's on your mind, try these out!

  • Do you often wonder how your friend would react if you brought up a difficult subject with them? To have a better idea of this, you could create a survey of hypothetical scenarios to which each of you write in how you would react and share with each other. This will help you each of you understand how the other would react to various situations and the reasoning behind their actions.

  • This is similar to the hypothetical scenarios survey, but with a fun twist. Create Madlibs of situations you and your friend may find yourselves in. Fill in the blanks with how you would feel and what actions you would take.

  • Sometimes you may think you know your friend, but they might behave and feel in a different way than you would have predicted. If you enjoy writing, you can write a short scene or story with your friend as the main character. After you're finished, let your friend edit the story with what they think they actually what have done in the situations you've put them in.

  • Since you'd like to establish a comfortable environment for you and your friend to give criticism to each other, you can exchange a compliment or something pleasant after each criticism you give each other. For instance, bake some cookies, and every time you critique your friend, give them a cookie.
